I was at the AI Engineer conference recently. A speaker dropped an acronym I didn't recognise — one of those three-letter ones that half the room clearly understood and the other half pretended to. Usually I'd jot it down, look it up afterwards, forget, and move on none the wiser.

Instead, in the corner of my laptop screen, the definition quietly appeared. No interruption, no "sorry, what's that mean again?". The talk kept going. I kept up.

That was the first time my retinue actually paid off.

## Granola finishes the meeting. I want help during it.

Apps like Granola do a great job of transcribing meetings and spitting out follow-up notes — action items, summaries, decisions. Really useful. But all of that value lands *after* the meeting is over, when the moment has already passed.

What I've consistently wanted is the bit *during* the call. When someone makes a claim that doesn't sound right — verify it. When a question comes up and no-one in the room knows the answer — find out. When someone says *"we'll circle back next week once we've figured out X"* — what if we didn't need to? What if X could be figured out in the next ninety seconds, while the meeting is still live?

Post-meeting summaries are the consolation prize. The real win is real-time.

## What the word actually means

I hadn't come across the word *retinue* before building this. It turns out to mean the group of advisors a royal or noble travelled with. Not a single chief of staff — an entourage. A merchant advisor who'd weigh in on the economics, a scholar who'd check the history, a priest who'd give the moral angle, a diplomat who'd read the politics. Each with their own lens, each whispering a different perspective as the king sat in a chamber making a decision.

Every monarch had one. Almost nobody else did.

That's the experience I want to give everyone. An always-on advisory council, each with their own speciality, quietly feeding you the information they've gone and gathered while you listen to whoever's speaking. Technology has a habit of taking things that were once the preserve of a tiny elite — personal drivers, personal trainers, personal chefs — and making them available to anyone with a device. A retinue of advisors has been the missing one.

## What Retinue actually does

It listens to the audio of whatever you're in — a meeting, a talk, a podcast, a one-on-one. It detects the moments worth acting on: an unfamiliar acronym, a factual claim that deserves checking, a question no-one in the room can answer. And it dispatches agents to go and look those things up while the conversation carries on.

The results show up on the side of your screen. Not as interruptions, just as options. You glance when you want to; you ignore when you don't.

The precursor was [Critiq](/essays/critiq-realtime-retort-2025/), a hackathon project I built last June in a phone booth at the Granola office — an iPhone app that detected logical contradictions in live audio. That was proof the live-listening piece worked. Retinue extends the idea from one type of advisor (a logician) to a whole entourage.

## So what

The retinue used to be the sign you were a king. Now it's a laptop and an API key. Not because it makes meetings shorter — it doesn't — but because it stops the *"we'll circle back next week"* moments from being the norm.

Every round trip you avoid is a week you get back.
